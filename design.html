LRSeat

Challenge
Redesign a public seating area at Northwestern using only corruaged cardboard.

Solution
LRSeat - modular chair/table combo

Process
We picked a space in Tech (see video below to see the space) where the seating - a few couches - is never maximized because students are often unwilling to sit with strangers and end up taking over the space. We also found that the space was not ideal for students who were trying to study, do work, or eat there between classes. We decided to create a modular chair and table piece that could be placed every-other, ensuring that a student could not spread out and take over multiple spaces, and also giving them space on a table to put their items, instead of on the seat next to them.

*This video was created in Photoshop

  • Class: Industrial Design Projects
  • Date: Winter 2016
  • Skills: Laser Cutting, Premiere Pro
  • Role: Design, Laser Cutting, Assembly, Video

NU Day

Challenge
How might we engage Northwestern students in contributing to a positive community?

Solution
NU Day app
NU Day is similar to a current app called Yik Yak, although NU Day requires students to log in with their Northwestern ID so that their posts are not anonymous. The idea is that students will post positive challenges, ideas, and other random things (there's a list of suggested themes within the app) that will encourage other students to part-take in these positive activities, or at least get students thinking about them.

iDonate

Problem
Donation centers often receive too many of the same item and not enough of the things they actually need. How can we connect college students with donation centers so that things don't go to waste?

Solution
iDonate - Donation app for Northwestern students

Process
Northwestern, and many other colleges, often have Facebook groups where students can sell unused or unwanted items. However, many items tend not to be sold and end up in the trash at the end of the year instead, so our team wanted to find a way to get those items to where they are needed.

We conducted user interviews with current sites like the Salvation Army and Donate Stuff to see what users did or did not like about the interface. Students also told us they'd like to know where their donated items are going (a feature neither site had) and would like the convienence of the center picking up the items.

With this feedback in mind, we created the app iDonate. With iDonate, you enter your zipcode and then are given a map of donation centers in your area. You can filter by which centers need what types of items, and the user can view a description of each center. There is also an option for pick-up or drop-off of goods.

Donation center workers can also use the app to post which items they are in need of, as well as update information about their center.
